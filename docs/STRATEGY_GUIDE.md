# Strategy Guide

This guide assumes you already know the basic rules.

The goal here is not just to survive a level. It is to play cleanly, stay in control, and improve your times.

## The Core Mindset

New players usually think the game is about chasing the ball.

It is not.

The real skill is shaping the playfield before the ball needs it.

The ball is just the moving test signal. You are building the track under it.

The best runs usually come from players who think one step ahead:

- where the ball will bounce next
- which relay is currently active
- which edge is usable
- whether a barrier is actually a problem or just visual noise

## Read The Live Objective First

Before you move anything, identify:

- the room the ball is currently in
- the current live relay or goal
- any blocked sides on the destination room

If you skip this and start dragging windows immediately, you often create connections that look right but can never work because the active room is closed on the side you are using.

## Build Short, Clean Routes

The easiest mistake is making one giant cluster of overlapping rooms.

That can work, but it often makes the ball harder to read and harder to control.

A better pattern is:

- connect only the rooms you need right now
- keep the route short
- keep the bounce space simple

Simple routes are easier to read, and they also make it easier to predict when the ball will reach a relay.

## Visibility Is Part Of The Puzzle

Relays and goals do not count just because the ball touched their world position.

If the live target is hidden behind another front-most room:

- the hit will not score
- the route will not advance
- you need to uncover that room or bring it back to the front

This means giant “cover everything” overlaps are weaker than they look. They may help you inspect geometry with xray, but they can also hide the exact relay or goal you need to clear.

## Use Walls As Steering

The ball is always bouncing.

That means walls are not just obstacles. They are steering surfaces.

Good players use room placement to change bounce angles:

- slide a room closer to catch a ball on the next rebound
- narrow the open lane so the ball reaches the relay sooner
- pull rooms apart to trap the ball in a smaller connected shape while you fix the next segment

If a route feels random, it usually means the connected shape is too large.

## Barriers: Clear Only What Matters

Do not shoot every barrier on instinct.

Ask one question first:

Does this barrier actually block the path I want?

If the answer is no, leave it alone.

Useful barrier priorities:

- clear barriers between the ball and the live relay
- clear barriers hiding a relay bonus you actually plan to collect
- clear barriers in a goal room only if they truly block the goal line

Low-value barrier clears:

- shooting side-room clutter while the ball is somewhere else
- cleaning a room that is not live yet
- over-clearing a room when one lane would have been enough

## Side Locks Change Everything

Later levels become much easier once you stop thinking of rooms as rectangles and start thinking of them as doors.

A room may be near the ball and still be useless if the contact edge is blocked.

When side locks are present:

- check the edge rails before you commit to a placement
- favor routes that use obviously open sides
- avoid “almost connected” layouts that rely on a blocked edge

If a route should work but the ball keeps bouncing back, this is the first thing to check.

## When To Use Bridge Pulse

Do not spend `Bridge Pulse` just because you have charges.

The best uses are:

- a locked edge is the only thing preventing an immediate relay hit
- the ball is already approaching the correct contact point
- a rebuild would cost more time than the charge is worth

Bad uses:

- spending it with no clear route ready
- triggering it while the ball is far away from the blocked connection
- using it to cover up a bad layout you could fix cleanly

Treat pulse as a tempo tool, not a panic button.

## Bonus Priorities

Not every bonus is worth chasing.

### Relay Bonus

This one is the easiest to misplay because it can disappear when the ball leaves the room.

Go for it when:

- the bonus sits naturally on the path to the relay
- you already have the ball contained in the relay room
- grabbing it will not delay the live objective too much

Skip it when:

- the ball is about to leave the room
- you would have to rebuild the route just to touch it
- the level pace matters more than the extra score

### Ambient Score Bonus

Take it if it is near the route already. Do not detour hard for it unless you are replaying casually for score.

### Ambient Charge Bonus

Usually worth taking if it is convenient. Charges can save late-level runs.

### Ambient Time Bonus

This is strongest when you are pushing medal times. If it is close to the route, it is often worth a small detour.

## Medal Runs Need Different Decisions

If you are chasing gold, you should play differently than if you are just trying to clear the level.

For time runs:

- build direct routes, not safe routes
- ignore low-value barriers
- take only high-value bonuses
- save pulse for time-critical blocked entries
- keep rooms readable instead of clever

Gold runs usually come from efficiency, not style.

## Use Overlap Xray Intentionally

The xray overlay is not just a visual trick. It is useful information.

Use it when:

- you want to inspect a barrier layout without moving the lower room away
- you need to understand which blocked edge is actually underneath
- you want to line up a route while keeping two rooms overlapped

The xray is best for structure. Do not rely on it as a permanent play mode. It is a quick inspection tool.

Just remember:

- xray helps you read hidden barriers and side locks
- xray does not let you score a hidden relay or goal

## Control The Ball Before You Extend The Route

One strong habit is to stabilize the current room before you reach forward.

That means:

- make the current connected shape manageable
- keep the ball bouncing somewhere predictable
- then extend toward the next relay

Trying to build the full chain too early often creates a huge connected region where the ball becomes harder to intercept.

## Late-Level Play

Higher levels pile up several pressure sources:

- more rooms
- more blocked sides
- more barriers
- faster ball speed
- smaller ball size

At that point, the winning approach is usually:

1. reduce the shape the ball can move in
2. clear only the critical blockers
3. build one clean step to the live relay
4. use pulse only when it converts directly into progress

Trying to make the whole board “look nice” is slower than just making the live path work.

## If You Get Lost Mid-Run

Use this recovery checklist:

1. Which room owns the ball right now?
2. Which room is live right now?
3. Which side of that room is open?
4. Is a barrier truly blocking the route?
5. Would a pulse solve this faster than a rebuild?

That usually gets you back into control quickly.

## Best Habits To Build

- Read the live relay before moving anything.
- Keep the connected route as small as possible.
- Treat barriers as path problems, not cleanup chores.
- Respect blocked edges first.
- Spend pulse only when it buys immediate progress.
- Take bonuses that fit the route, not bonuses that fight it.
- Play for clean geometry if you want faster times.

That is the current skill ceiling of the game.
